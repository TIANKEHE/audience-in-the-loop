Audience in the Loop:

Viewer Feedback-Driven Content Creation in Micro-drama Production on Social Media

Gengchen Cao*¹ · Tianke He*² · Yixuan Liu³ · RAY LC³⁺
¹Tsinghua University | ²Sichuan University of Media and Communication | ³City University of Hong Kong
arXiv Full Paper (PDF)

Abstract

The popularization of social media has led to increasing consuming of narrative content in byte-sized formats. Such micro-dramas contain fast-pace action and emotional cliffs, particularly attractive to emerging Chinese markets in platforms like Douyin and Kuaishou.

Through 28 semi-structured interviews, we found that the workflow leads to writers iteratively adapting to storylines in response to real-time audience feedback. This work reveals audience interaction as a new paradigm for collaborative creative processes on social media.

Research Questions

RQ1 / WORKFLOW

What emerging roles and production workflows have micro-drama creators adopted in production?

RQ2 / COLLABORATION

How do creators collaborate with audiences to iteratively shape the plots and characters?

Related Work

The Emergence of Micro-dramas

Characterized by 1-3 minute episodes and platform affordances like vertical screens and algorithmic recommendations.

Platform-Shaped Practices

Unlike traditional three-act structures, micro-dramas use a "hook-driven" narrative model shaped by algorithmic incentives.

Audience Participation

Explores how implicit signals (memes, keywords) are integrated beyond simple likes and shares.

Creative Labor Constraints

Analyzes algorithmic risk labor, creative homogenization, and the asymmetric labor between creators and viewers.

Methods

28 Participants
9 yrs Max Experience
Snowball Sampling Method

Recruited 24 practitioners and 4 traditional screenwriters across Douyin, TikTok, and Tencent. Inductive thematic coding was applied to interview transcripts by three independent researchers until saturation.

Key Results

Evolution of Creative Workflow (RQ1)

  • Industrial Shift: Transition from long-cycle linear progression to a compressed "test-and-broadcast" model.
  • Blurred Boundaries: Emergence of new roles like Traffic Operation Managers and Localization Screenwriters.
  • Active Collaborators: Audiences now influence actor screen time and plot development through a closed feedback loop.

Iterative Mode Driven by Feedback (RQ2)

  • Dual Basis: Reliance on both quantitative metrics (completion rates) and textual feedback (bullet screens).
  • Permeation: Feedback affects every stage—from early topic selection to late-stage series continuation decisions.
  • The " handsome male lead" effect: Real-world cases show significant interaction surges when adjusting plots based on viewer comments.

⚠️ Pitfalls of Overreliance

Creators face doubts about data authenticity (fake traffic), inability to interpret irony via AI, and creative fatigue leading to team disbandment.

Discussion

Reconstruction of Narrative Interactivity: Micro-dramas are participatory cultural products. Platform algorithms amplify implicit audience signals, forming a distributed interactive system between audiences, creators, and platforms.

Personalization Risks: While data feedback helps alignment, it risks content homogenization and marginalizing the "silent majority."

Design Implications

  • Provide transparent tools for screenwriters to interpret audience signals.
  • Establish mechanisms to protect minority opinions and creative diversity.
  • Shift evaluation from short-term data to long-term satisfaction metrics.

Conclusion

This study uncovers an "audience-in-the-loop" mechanism that transforms writing from an author-centered activity into a sociotechnical negotiation. We emphasize the need to balance audience influence with narrative diversity and creative autonomy.
