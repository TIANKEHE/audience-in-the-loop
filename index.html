Abstract

The popularization of social media has led to increasing consuming of narrative content in byte-sized formats. Such micro-dramas contain fast-pace action and emotional cliffs. Through 28 semi-structured interviews, we found that the workflow leads to writers iteratively adapting to storylines in response to real-time audience feedback.

Discussion

Reconstruction of Narrative Interactivity

Micro-dramas are no longer pre-scripted linear works, but rather participatory cultural products that are constantly reshaped through comments, memes, and hashtags. The interactivity of micro-dramas is driven by implicit audience signals, forming a distributed interactive system where audience feedback penetrates the entire creative process, and platform algorithms amplify these signals to construct a dynamic narrative community of "audiences-creators-platforms".

Personalization and Risks

While data feedback helps creators align with audience expectations, it may also lead to content homogenization and confine audiences to familiar narrative frameworks, reducing their exposure to diverse perspectives. In addition, the strategic guidance of platforms and creators may make audience feedback "unnatural", and the discourse power is mostly held by a small number of active users, easily marginalizing the silent majority. Meanwhile, sensitive personal information in comments may be captured, posing user privacy leakage risks.

🛠 Design Implications

It is necessary to provide screenwriters with more transparent tools for interpreting audience signals to maintain narrative coherence during rapid iteration. Establish mechanisms to protect minority opinions and ensure the diversity of the creative ecosystem. Transform evaluation indicators from short-term interactive data to long-term satisfaction metrics, and build configurable feedback systems to allow creators to explore alternative narrative scenarios based on different feedback perspectives.

Conclusion

This study uncovers a data-responsive, market-oriented distributed creation model in micro-drama production. This "audience-in-the-loop" mechanism reshapes the creative identity of creators and transforms writing from an author-centered activity into a sociotechnical process of negotiation.
The findings provide a new perspective for understanding collaborative creative labor in the platformized era, and also offer design insights for the sustainable development of the micro-drama ecosystem, emphasizing the need to balance audience influence, narrative diversity, and creative autonomy in platform design.
